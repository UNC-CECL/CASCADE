## SUMMARY STATISTICS FOR MANUSCRIPT
# ========================================================================

**Study Period:** 1978-1997 (19 years)

**Total Tropical Cyclones Affecting North Carolina:** ~20 systems

**Total with Significant Hatteras Impacts:** 11 systems (55% of NC impacts)

**Impact Classification:**
- Major (Cat 2+ conditions, extensive damage): 3 storms (27%)
- Moderate (measurable erosion/surge): 5 storms (45%)  
- Minor (peripheral impacts): 3 storms (27%)

**Temporal Distribution:**
- Years with zero impacts: 13 years (68%)
- Years with 1 impact: 3 years (16%)
- Years with 2+ impacts: 3 years (16%)
- Longest quiet period: 5 consecutive years (1987-1990)

**Storm Days vs. Background Days:**
- Estimated days under significant storm conditions: ~11 days
- Total days in study period: 6,935 days
- Percentage of time under storm forcing: 0.16%
- Percentage of time under background forcing: 99.84%

**Physical Impact Range:**
- Storm surge: 3-10 ft
- Maximum winds at Hatteras: 74-98 mph (Category 1-2)
- Documented damage: $8M to $2B per event (1996 dollars)

**CASCADE Modeling Implications:**
- Recommended storms for inclusion: 8 events (3 major + 5 moderate)
- Optional storms for sensitivity testing: 3 events (minor impacts)
- Background erosion should represent 85-95% of total change
- Storm contribution should represent 5-15% of total change

"During the 1978-1997 study period, Hatteras Island experienced 11 
significant tropical cyclone impacts, including three major hurricanes 
(Gloria 1985, Emily 1993, Fran 1996) and five moderate tropical storms 
or hurricanes (Josephine 1984, Bob 1991, Danielle 1992, Felix 1995, 
Bertha 1996). This represented approximately 11 days of significant 
storm conditions out of 6,935 total days (0.16% of the study period). 
The 1978-1997 period experienced below-average tropical cyclone activity 
relative to the long-term climatological record, with 68% of years (13 
of 19) having minimal to no storm impacts on Hatteras Island."


**For Results/Discussion:**

"The calibrated model attributed 90% of observed shoreline change to 
persistent background processes and 10% to episodic storm events. This 
partition is consistent with the temporal dominance of continuous 
forcing (99.84% of time) over discrete storm events (0.16% of time) 
during the relatively quiescent 1978-1997 period. In contrast, more 
active periods (e.g., 1999-2005) would require different partitioning 
to reflect increased storm contributions."
